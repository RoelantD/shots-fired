Agentic AI is not just changing how software gets built. New models drop faster than I can form an opinion about the last one. I work in AI full time. If I cannot keep up, a regular consumer has absolutely no chance. And honestly? That might be fine.

---

Last week there were three major model releases, or four. I have genuinely lost count. There was something with "turbo" in the name, something with a version number that skipped several integers for no obvious reason, and at least one announcement that was framed as a breakthrough but turned out to be a slightly better score on a benchmark nobody outside a research lab cares about.

I track this stuff for a living. I speak at conferences about it. I have paid subscriptions to more AI products than I care to admit publicly. And I still spent twenty minutes last Tuesday trying to remember whether the model I was using was the new one or the previous new one that had replaced the one before that.

This is the state of the industry in 2026. And it is exhausting.

## The naming situation is out of control

Let's just acknowledge it. The naming conventions in this industry are a disaster. We have models with version numbers that suggest a logical sequence but have no relationship to capability jumps. We have "mini" and "pro" and "turbo" and "preview" appended in combinations that mean different things at different vendors. We have models that share a name but perform completely differently depending on which API endpoint you hit.

OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, Mistral, Cohere: each has its own versioning logic, its own tier naming, its own way of deprecating things without quite saying they are deprecated. There is no shared vocabulary. There is no standard. There is just a constant stream of new names arriving in your feed like a river that never runs dry and never runs clear.

A developer trying to make a sensible architectural decision about which model to build on faces a genuine problem. By the time they finish evaluating options, half of them have been superseded. A regular person trying to decide which subscription to pay for has even less to go on. The benchmarks are gamed. The marketing is identical. Everyone is the most capable model for most tasks. The YOLO response to this, increasingly popular and arguably correct, is to stop choosing entirely. Set your tool to "auto," let a router decide, and never look at the model name again. You do not choose which server handles your web request. Maybe you should not be choosing this either.

> "Everyone is the most capable model for most tasks. One of the main reasons there are currently capacity challenges"

## Here is what nobody wants to say out loud

The models are already very good. Not perfect. Not finished. But for the vast majority of real-world tasks that real people and real businesses need done, the difference between the top five models on any given week is marginal. You are not going to write a better email with GPT-5.5 than you were with GPT-5.4. Your code is not going to be architecturally sounder because you switched from Opus 4.5 to Opus 4.6. Your summary is not going to be meaningfully sharper.

The gains are real but they are narrowing at the edges that matter for most use cases. What is growing fast is the surface area of features, integrations, context windows, and pricing tiers, none of which help a regular user understand what they are actually paying for.

The release velocity is not primarily about making the product better for you. It is about owning the narrative, maintaining developer mindshare, and making sure nobody writes "Company X is falling behind." It is a marketing cadence dressed up as an innovation cadence. Anthropic's Mythos launch this month is the purest recent example: the model leaked from an unsecured data cache before it was even named, got rebranded from Capybara to something considerably more dramatic, and was announced not as a product release but as an exclusive preview for a twelve-partner security consortium with a name "Project Glasswing" that sounds like a Tom Clancy subplot. Within two weeks, the NSA was reportedly using it and an unauthorized group had reportedly gained access to it. Whether Mythos actually represents a step change in capability is still genuinely unclear. But the launch playbook was immaculate. That does not mean the improvements are fake. It means the frequency is disconnected from the actual pace of things becoming meaningfully better for users.

## So does it still matter which one you choose?

Less and less. That is the honest answer.

For highly specific technical tasks, long-horizon reasoning, complex code generation, multimodal work, there are still real differences worth caring about. If you are building a product on top of a model, those differences can matter and it is worth doing the evaluation work properly.

But for most people, using most AI tools, most of the time? Pick the one that fits into your existing workflow and budget, but stop chasing the release notes. The energy you spend staying current on model versions is energy you are not spending on actually using the tools to get things done.

And whatever you do: do not put all your eggs in one basket.

The vendor you are fully committed to today can change pricing tomorrow, deprecate the model you built on next quarter, or simply fall behind while you are locked into their ecosystem. This industry has not been around long enough to have earned that kind of loyalty. Use multiple tools. Stay portable. Treat these models as kettle, not as cows.

> "This industry has not been around long enough to have earned that kind of loyalty."

The models are good enough. They have been for a while. The race now is not about who builds the best tool, it is about who gets to put their name on the category. That fight is still very much on. Just do not let it distract you from actually getting things done.
